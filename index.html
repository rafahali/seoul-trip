Tips · Seoul

The handful of things that make Seoul easy. Read once, then move like you live here.

Maps: use Naver Map (or KakaoMap). Google driving navigation doesn't work in Korea. Search in English, it mostly resolves.

Taxis: grab the KakaoT app, the local Uber, cheap and everywhere. Most of this menu is a $4–6 ride from the hotel.

Subway: Apgujeong is on Line 3, which reaches the Jongno-area spots, but budget a transfer for Euljiro, Sindang, and Dongmyo (or just grab a KakaoT taxi, $4–6). Buy a T-money card at any convenience store and tap on and off.

Cash: needed for the tents, flea markets, and the nogari alley. Cards work everywhere else.

Don't tip. Anywhere. Korea doesn't do it and it reads as odd. The price is the price.

Translation: download Papago, it beats Google Translate for Korean and has a camera mode that reads menus live.

Reservations: the top restaurants and bars run on the CatchTable app (English, foreign cards). Reserve buttons land on each spot's date screen. For a 5-top this close in, turn on the in-app waitlist, drops hit 1–2 days out. Mingles needs a deposit and Evett needs an email for five.

Jjimjilbang: shower fully nude before the baths, no phones in the bath area, then put on the matching pajamas for the co-ed floors. Yes, do the body scrub.

Clubs: carry passports, and pre-book a table so the door is a non-issue for a group of five.

Play it smart · a week in Saigon

It's a menu, not a schedule. But a few things reward planning, sequence these and the week opens up. Tap + on any card to drop it into your Plan.

Start the clock day one
  • Want a suit (or custom shoes) made? Decide and get measured the day you land. Made-to-measure needs a mid-week fitting and an end-of-week pickup, so it only works if you start early.
  • Anan Saigon: email about two weeks out, and the whole table orders the same tasting menu, so agree on it first.
  • Night motorbike food tour: sells out for groups of five, book it roughly a week ahead.
Build the week around these
  • The big tasting menus (Akuna and friends) often close Sunday and Monday, slot your fine-dining night midweek.
  • Cu Chi tunnels is a half-day morning trip, go to the Ben Duoc entrance on a weekday for far fewer buses.
  • The Mekong is worth an overnight in Can Tho, not a day trip, the floating market peaks 5:30–7:30am and you have to sleep there to catch it.
Catch these at the right hour
  • Dawn: the Tao Dan birdmen (~6:30am), the Bo Ne sizzling breakfast (5–11am), the floating market.
  • Dusk: ca phe bet on the Palace lawns, Turtle Lake, the rooftops.
  • After 7pm: the Vinh Khanh snail street and the Ho Thi Ky food alley come alive.

Tips · Saigon

The handful of things that make Saigon easy. Read once, then move like you live here.

Get around on Grab. The local Uber for both cars and motorbikes, cheap, cashless, English. Google Maps works fine here for navigation, unlike Korea.

Crossing the street: walk slow and steady into the scooter flow and don't stop or bolt, they're built to flow around you. Hesitating is what gets you hit.

Cash + QR. Street food, snails, and markets are cash; keep small notes. Most bars and restaurants take cards or QR. No tipping expected, rounding up is plenty.

Addresses moved in 2025: the city renamed and merged wards, so listings show both old and new. Navigate by street name + district number (District 1, 3, 4, 5), which is what Grab and locals still use.

Coffee is strong and sweet. Order ca phe sua da (iced, condensed milk) as the default; bac xiu is the creamier version. It's rocket fuel, pace yourself.

Get the suit started day one. Made-to-measure needs a fitting mid-week and pickup at the end, so visit the tailor the day you land. Skip any "24-hour suit," it's glued, not made.

The toast is "Mot, hai, ba, DZO!" (one-two-three, yo!). Learn xin chao (hi), cam on (thanks), ngon (delicious). Drop them at a snail table and you've made friends.

Bui Vien rules: pay round-by-round in cash, confirm prices before ordering, watch the tab, and skip the nitrous balloons (now illegal and a police target). Do one night, on purpose.

Don't drink the tap water. Bottled only, and easy on street ice if your stomach's sensitive, though the cylindrical ice at proper spots is factory-made and fine.

June is rainy season: short, hard afternoon downpours that pass fast. Keep a poncho or duck into a cafe; mornings and evenings are usually clear.
